Week 14 | 05.11.22

bourriaud


It was interesting to read about the relationship between art and technology and the way it has been perceived over time. While there is always opportunity and new potential with technological advancements, as the reading notes, there is also a “darker side” to any new technologies, such as how these can be used for “subjugation as much as improvements to daily life.” Of course there is always the inequities associated with such developments and who gets to appreciate these technologies and who gets left out, as well as the false promise of “universality.” The “two-sided” nature of technology was also highlighted in the discussion about screens as being both a surface that “arrests light” such as in cinema, while also serving as a medium “on which information is written.”


I thought the discussion of the Law of Relocation was interesting because it felt relevant to those of use who are students and learning new media and how we can apply them to our own work, particularly coding. A line from the reading that stood out to me was “those who produce so-called 'computer graphic' images, by manipulating synthetic fractal and images, usually fall into the trap of illustration.” I find that a challenge for myself when learning new technologies, and in this class, coding in particular, is in taking something that may already exist (for example some source code that was written by someone else) and doing something completely new with it. Of course it is that much more rewarding the farther away your own work moves from the source code you may have started with.


I also thought the discussion of “synthetic imagery” was interesting, and how it differs from prior artistic mediums because it is no longer about the human hand or “gestures” as the reading states. I found it relevant to this class because what we have been doing is essentially in a way creating systems that can create images for us, rather than individually creating 100 different outputs on our own. In this way I thought that the line “the contemporary image is typified precisely by its generative power” was resonant, and it has been a challenging but cool change of pace to step back and let the code do the work for us. To close I think that the relationship between art and technology is one that I would love to continue to explore further, and the line from the reading that “art's function” is “to reverse the authority of technology in order to make ways of thinking, living, and seeing creative” is something that we can take with us as we continue to incorporate new media and new technologies into our own practices moving forward.
Week 13 | 05.04.22

mcluhan


I thought this week's reading provided a really interesting discussion of the scale of impact of automation on our lives and in the world. I think it was particularly relevant given the shifts that we all see today with respect to work and society, and education as well. One theme in the reading that struck me was the tension and contrast between the specialist, fragmented aspects of the mechanical age and the generalist, interconnected aspects of the electric age. In a previous UX class, we learned about the benefits of being a generalist versus a specialist in the field, which struck me because I used to think that specialists were more “valued”. However, this makes sense when thinking about our world today through the lens of of the electric age and it's “all-at-once” or interrelated/interdependent aspects, which reveal how knowledge cannot be isolated to one subject without an understanding of its relations to other subjects.


The discussion of learning and education in the context of the electric age was also interesting. I found it resonated particularly today and in the age of Covid, where people have reconsidered their careers or gone back to school, or took classes on their own using the many resources that are available to us now for self-learning even through YouTube or even Tik Tok. It is interesting to think about how Wikipedia and even Google search was discouraged or even prohibited from being used as a source for research in school, but now “learning how to Google” is actually skill that we rely on today. With so many resources available to us online, from tutorials to complete courses, it was interesting that the reading pointed out that teachers are “the largest employee group in the US and may well become the only group”, especially as teachers are historically undervalued and underpaid in schools today. It is interesting that the electric age has both created new things to learn but also allows us to learn new things by allowing us to access more information which then results in more new technologies, jobs, etc.


Something else that stood out to me was when McLuhan refers to the “specter” of joblessness and propertylessness in the electric age, which made me think of political discussions of joblessness and unemployment in America. The metric of unemployment is always used as a cudgel to “convince” voters that only one party is capable of creating jobs, and automation is demonized as taking away people's jobs, rather than viewed, as McLuhan describes, as a model for society. The discussion of “total interdependence” was also interesting to me, along with his discussion of war, and how it becomes “impracticable” with electric speed “because organic interdependence means that disruption of any part of the organism can prove fatal to the whole.” It makes me wonder when people may view or when politicians may think to brand automation as a tool to be leveraged rather than something to “panic” about.

I think people often mistake artificial intelligence or algorithms as being devoid of any human intervention, which I think is sometimes part of the design (like with big tech), however it was interesting to read about the many different ways in which automation is grounded and shaped by human forces behind the scenes. It also made me think about the irony of the idea of laziness or automation, especially in the context of Mladen Stilinovic's “Demand Full Laziness,” which juxtaposed images of laziness with all of the work that went into producing those images. The idea of a post-work society is fascinating and scary at the same time, as the Oblomo project showed, and I think something that is on a lot of people's minds is what jobs will be replaced by automation in the future, even coding. joselit


It was interesting to read about NFTs in the context of Marcel Duchamp's readymades. As someone who is still wrapping his head around NFTs, the contrast between NFTs and Duchamp's work provided a new perspective. A line that stood out to me was “With the readymade, Duchamp gave sculpture over to information on two registers: He desublimated matter by suspending it between two names, shovel and sculpture; and he abandoned objects for discourse.” The discussion of “information,” struck me in the context of how we are now living in the information age, and Duchamp's work was about ideas or “information” as opposed to about the materiality of the objects themselves. It was interesting to read then how NFTs are in a way the reverse, where digital files that don't exist as material objects in the physical world are assigned value.


It was also interesting to read about the discussion of matter as property, and the “aesthetics” of the relationships between the two. I was struck by the line about how “the NFT is a social contract that values property over material experience” and how “that contract can be broken.” It made me think about the popularity of NFTs and the question of their longevity and their precarious status. I feel that discussions of cryptocurrency are always divided between enthusiasts and those who are skeptical about their long term value and the idea of an NFT as a social contract that can be broken to me adds to my skeptical side, that if enough people begin to question the idea of valuing “property” over “material experience,” then maybe the popularity of NFTs will begin to wane as well, although of course, as the reading says, “the market will do what the market will do.”

reading response: information ages - hobart


I enjoyed this reading's discussion of the play in the context of information technology, and the distinction between what we think of as frivolous play versus something that allows us to engage with information in a deeper way. It was interesting to think about the idea of play as something that, in the words of Johan Huizinga, is governed by “rules freely accepted but absolutely binding” which at first seems like a contradiction, but makes sense in the context of computers which are governed by the “absolutely binding logical rules of information processing.”


I thought it was also interesting to think of computers as toys, because they have become relatively so simple to use that they allow us to remain ignorant of what goes on under the hood to power our everyday tasks. The historical context of computer technology reminds us of how quickly and how far they have come, as today we take for granted how much our lives and the “human experience”, as the reading puts it, has become digitized. The line “we engross ourselves in these activities simply because we can” struck me because of both the perils of how easily and readily we become consumed by our devices, social media, etc., but also because of the potential for new learning. The reading discusses how a deeper dimension of play is complexity, and how information in the contemporary age is an “ongoing, playful activity, time- and place-bound, teeming with local rather than universal truths.” This passage, and the discussion of “chaos” and “complexity” reminded me of prior readings and the idea of conditional design, which rely not on fixed, predictable outcomes but a process that also takes into account “inputs” from the environment.


Something that I took from the reading is how the advancement of technology allows us to do complex things in a simple way that almost becomes rote. I think about kids who grow up today not knowing life without computers, and it is interesting but also scary to think about how this merging of the human experience and technology is something that has become second nature to us. At the same time I think there is exciting potential for what can happen when “we engross ourselves in these activities simply because we can,” and these “activities” involve discovering new outcomes that we otherwise could not without the help of computers. Something that came to mind was how p5 makes it easier for us to create visual drawings using code, something that we might not otherwise do. I think it's cool to think about play as a tool for innovation and discovery.

e2 - instructions for paper


One of the biggest challenges I found in working on the instructions was controlling the variability in the outcomes. I think the more specific an outcome that I had in mind the more challenging it was to write the instructions in a way that another person would understand and interpret them the same way that I would. On the other hand I think it would be interesting to see how people might interpret a more open ended set of instructions, or perhaps some more poetic set of instructions like in Yoko Ono's Grapefruit. For my instructions, I decided to try and allow for some variability while still trying to create a sort of “template” or structure as a base for each execution.



Directions 1

1. Draw a “grid” of 5 dots by 5 dots in the middle of your paper (roughly the width of your hand). Do not connect any points yet.

2. Starting from the top left dot and working clockwise around the 4 sides of your grid, draw 3 more dots in between each of the dots you drew in Step 1, to create an “outline” of dots.

3. Choose a dot on the top edge of your grid. Draw a line from this dot to every dot on the opposite side of your grid.

4. Choose a dot on the right edge of your grid. Draw a line from this dot to each dot on the opposite side.

5. Repeat for the bottom edge and finally the left edge.



Directions 2

1. Draw a “grid” of 5 dots by 5 dots in the middle of your paper (roughly the width of your hand). Do not connect any points yet.

2. Starting from the top left dot and working only horizontally, draw 3 more dots in between each of the dots you drew in Step 1 (only horizontally). You should now have 5 rows of 17 dots.

3. Starting at the top left dot, connect each dot in in row 1 to another dot in row 2.

4. Repeat step 3, connecting every dot in row 2 to another dot in row 3, row 3 to row 4, and row 4 to row 5.



Directions 3

1. Draw a 4x4 grid of dots in the middle of your paper (a little smaller than the width of your hand).

2. Starting from the top left dot and working only horizontally, draw 3 more dots in between each of the dots you drew in Step 1 (only horizontally). You should now have 4 rows of 13 dots.

3. Draw a diagonal line connecting one dot in row 1 to another dot in row 2.

4. Draw a diagonal line going in the opposite direction connecting the same dot in row 2 to another dot in row 3 to create a zig zag.

5. Repeat step 4 connecting the dot in row 3 to a dot in row 4.

6. Connect the outermost dots to create an outline of your grid.

7. Draw parallel zig-zag lines only to the right of your original zig-zag, using the dots you drew to evenly space the lines. Each line should be contained within the outline of your grid.
